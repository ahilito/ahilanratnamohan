Raumdeuter

football| dance | theatre | abstract yet playful | choreographic research




In essence, football is the organisation of 22 bodies in a space.


In Raumdeuter*, Ahilan Ratnamohan continues his choreographic exploration of football, by zooming in on space and how it is physically created.  Together with a cast of 6 footballer-performers, he explores how space can be organised through and by movement.  


In an open theatre space, scene sûr scene, using the codes of football and in close, yet soft collaboration with the audience, 6 footballer-performers explore the possibilities of organising space through the codes of football.  In its essence, football is the organisation of 22 bodies in a space, constantly moving and re-organising in reaction to the other bodies and a ball.  With Raumdeuter, Ahilan wishes to explore how these movement principles can be translated to the stage if the ball and goals no longer remain.  But also, beyond movement, what are the other codes for organising space beyond movement. Sound?  Light?  Clothing?  Bodies?


But also, to go a step further than movement and zoom in other codes that organise football-space, such as sound,  light, clothing and bodies.


Raumdeuter attempts to put structural and formational concepts of football into practice with an audience, translated in an abstract, yet playful manner. 

Raumdeuter attempts to take technical movements, i.e. skills with the ball, and without, as triggers for spatial re-organisation.

Raumdeuter attempts to take the feeling of being in a match, of moving between and avoiding bodies, and explore it in a choreographic context.


The idea for the piece grew out of two performance experiences Ahilan had in the last years: I remember watching as “Football X Fashion”, -  a staged catwalk, in which all of the silhouettes were made from upcycled football jerseys and displayed by a group of 50 footballer-models, which we made in 2024 - entered its final stage. As we transported the catwalk to a third and final location, the audience ran, literally like a stampede, to get a view of what boded to be the final act… The suspense and energy and anticipation which imbued them blew my mind.

Years later, I went to watch “Holy” by Martha Canga Antonio, which although it took place in a black box, succeeded in creating a similar effect. We, the audience, were so keen to discover the next scene, and site of performance, that we were often ambling, if not running, to catch it.  


*Raumdeuter is a German language football term made famous by Thomas Müller around 2011.  It translates roughly as “space interpreter”.
